# Chickpea / Gram (Cicer arietinum)

## Overview
Chickpea (locally called "gram" or "chana") is Pakistan's most important pulse crop and a key source of protein for the population. It is a Rabi legume crop grown mostly under rainfed conditions in Punjab and Balochistan. It fixes atmospheric nitrogen, improving soil fertility for subsequent crops.

## Season
- **Type:** Rabi (winter/dry season crop)
- **Sowing:** October – November
- **Harvesting:** March – April
- **Duration:** ~120–150 days

## Growing Regions in Pakistan
- **Punjab IV (Pulses-Wheat):** Thal, D.G. Khan, Bhakkar, Khushab — rainfed areas
- **Balochistan:** Quetta, Pishin, Zhob — rainfed highlands
- **Sindh III:** Limited canal-irrigated areas
- **NWFP (KP) III:** Pulses-wheat rainfed belt

## Ideal Soil Conditions
- **Nitrogen (N):** 20–50 kg/ha (low — fixes own N via rhizobia; starter dose only)
- **Phosphorus (P):** 60–90 kg/ha (high P demand for nodulation and yield)
- **Potassium (K):** Generally not required unless soil test shows deficiency
- **Rhizobium inoculation:** Seed inoculation with Mesorhizobium ciceri strongly recommended
- **pH:** 6.0–8.0 (tolerates wide range; sensitive to acidic soils below 5.5)
- **Soil type:** Light sandy loam to loam — good drainage critical

## Climate Requirements
- **Temperature:** 15–25°C (cool and dry — cannot tolerate frost at flowering)
- **Humidity:** 30–60% (low humidity essential — high humidity causes disease)
- **Rainfall:** 400–600 mm total; grows well in rainfed conditions with 300–500 mm seasonal
- **Critical:** No rain at flowering and podding stage — promotes Ascochyta blight

## Fertilizer Application Timing
- At sowing: all P and starter N (20–25 kg/ha)
- No further N applications — rhizobia fix nitrogen naturally
- Seed inoculation with Rhizobium culture before sowing

## Common Diseases & Pests
- **Ascochyta blight (Ascochyta rabiei):** Most devastating — favored by rain during crop season
- **Fusarium wilt:** Soil-borne, causes wilting; use resistant varieties
- **Botrytis gray mold:** In humid, cool conditions
- **Pod borer (Helicoverpa armigera):** Eats developing seeds inside pods
- **Aphids:** Transmit viruses, reduce yield in warm spells

## Warning Signs
- Rain during February–March (flowering) → Ascochyta blight explosion, spray fungicide immediately
- Yellowing despite good soil → check nodulation (dig roots, look for pink nodules)
- No nodules or white nodules → Rhizobium not working, N deficiency
- Lodging before harvest → excess moisture or nitrogen

## Soil Tips
- Avoid waterlogged or heavy clay fields — chickpea roots rot easily
- Deep tillage breaks hardpan, improves root penetration for rainfed conditions
- Avoid applying excess nitrogen — suppresses Rhizobium nodulation
- Rotate with wheat, cotton — breaks disease cycle

## Rotation Partners
- **Previous crop:** Wheat, cotton, maize
- **Next crop:** Wheat, cotton (chickpea leaves residual N — reduces next crop's N need by 20–30 kg/ha)
- Excellent green manure if ploughed in

## Desi vs Kabuli Types
- **Desi (small, dark, rough coat):** 85–90% of Pakistan's production; drought tolerant; higher protein
- **Kabuli (large, cream, smooth coat):** Premium export market; lower yield, needs more moisture

## Alternative Crops If Conditions Borderline
- **Lentil:** If soil is heavier and rainfall slightly higher
- **Mung bean:** If season shifts to early Kharif
- **Wheat:** If rainfall is too low even for chickpea

## Market & Economic Notes
- Pakistan is both producer and importer of chickpea (domestic deficit)
- Kabuli chickpea exported to Middle East and Europe — good foreign exchange earner
- Price highly volatile — linked to monsoon performance in rainfed areas
- Government promotes pulse cultivation to reduce import dependency and improve nutrition
- Intercropping with wheat practiced in Thal — risk management strategy
